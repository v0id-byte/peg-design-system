## Slide 3 — What even is a stablecoin?

**ON-SCREEN**
- **A crypto token pegged 1:1 to real money — usually the US dollar.**
- 1 USDT ≈ $1.00
- USDT + USDC ≈ **84%** of the market · total ≈ **$306 billion** (end of 2025)
- *(Not all are stable: the algorithmic coin UST collapsed in 2022.)*

**SAY**
"Most cryptocurrencies, like Bitcoin, swing wildly in price — which makes them useless for everyday payments. A stablecoin fixes that by pegging its value to something stable, almost always one US dollar. So one unit of USDT is *meant* to always equal one dollar. Two coins dominate: Tether's USDT and Circle's USDC — together about 84% of a market worth roughly 306 billion dollars at the end of 2025. But 'stable' is a promise, not a guarantee. In 2022 a different kind of stablecoin, an algorithmic one called UST, broke its peg and collapsed in days. We'll come back to that."

**SOURCE** — Market cap & 49% YoY growth: Decrypt / DeFiLlama (2025). Market share: Brookings (2025); Knowledge@Wharton (2026).

## Slide 4 — How does it stay at exactly $1?

**ON-SCREEN**
- **Reserves + the right to redeem + arbitrage.**
- $1 in → 1 token minted   ·   1 token returned → $1 out
- *The peg holds only if people trust the reserves are really there.*

**SAY**
"How does a private token hold its value at exactly one dollar? Two mechanisms. First, **reserves**: for every token in circulation, the issuer claims to hold one real dollar — in cash or safe assets — that you can always redeem. Second, **arbitrage**: if the price ever drifts above a dollar, traders create new tokens and sell them for a small profit, pushing the price back down; if it dips below a dollar, they buy cheap and redeem for a full dollar, pushing it back up. So the peg is actually held in place by ordinary traders chasing tiny profits. The whole thing rests on one thing: trust that the reserves are real."

**SOURCE** — 1:1 reserve requirement: GENIUS Act (2025); HK *Stablecoins Ordinance* (100% high-quality liquid reserves).

## Slide 5 — The hidden money machine

**ON-SCREEN**
- **So how do issuers make money?**
- Your dollars → **US Treasuries** → issuer keeps the interest
- \+ a small **0.1–0.2%** fee to mint or redeem
- *Profit shrinks fast if interest rates fall.*

**SAY**
"Here's the part most people miss. If the token is basically free to use, how does the company behind it make money? When you hand over a dollar, they don't let it sit idle — they buy US government bonds, called Treasuries, which pay interest, and they keep that interest for themselves. They also charge a small fee, around 0.1 to 0.2%, to create or cash out coins. With *hundreds of billions* of dollars in reserves, that interest adds up to enormous profits. The catch: this only works while interest rates are high. If the central bank cuts rates, that easy money shrinks fast."

**SOURCE** — Fee structure & rate-dependence: 36Kr stablecoin analysis (2025). Reserve-yield model: BIS, FSI Briefs No. 27 (2025).

## Slide 6 — ⭐ The big "why": stablecoins, US debt & the dollar

**ON-SCREEN**
- **More stablecoins → more buyers of US debt → cheaper US borrowing + wider dollar reach.**
- Your $ → USDT → **US Treasuries**
- *Tether's Treasury buying is now big enough to move interest rates.*

**SAY**
"Now the real reason governments care. Because reserves are mostly US Treasuries, every dollar that flows into stablecoins becomes *new demand for US government debt*. That's a gift to the US — it helps fund the deficit and keeps borrowing cheap. Researchers have found Tether's Treasury buying is now large enough to actually move interest rates: for every 1% it gains in the short-term Treasury market, one-month yields drop by almost four basis points. And because these are *dollar* tokens used all over the world, they quietly push the dollar deeper into global digital payments. That's why the US doesn't fight stablecoins — it welcomes them."

**SOURCE** — Interest-rate effect: Ante, Saggu & Fiedler (2025), cited in 36Kr (2025).

## Slide 7 — The global race (and China's seat at the table)

**ON-SCREEN**
- **Everyone wants in.**
- 🇺🇸 GENIUS Act (Jul 2025) · 🇭🇰 Ordinance (Aug 1 2025, HKMA license) · 🇪🇺 MiCA
- Even **JD.com** & **Ant Group** moved toward Hong Kong stablecoins

**SAY**
"So a global race is on. The US passed the GENIUS Act in July 2025. Hong Kong's Stablecoins Ordinance took effect on August 1st, 2025 — now you need a license from Hong Kong's monetary authority just to issue one. Even Chinese tech giants like JD.com and Ant Group announced plans to enter, mainly aiming at cross-border payments. There's a fascinating split here: mainland China bans crypto trading, while Hong Kong is being used as a controlled testing ground. This is exactly the world I glimpsed from the bottom up — ordinary people reaching for USDT to move money — now being fought over by the biggest players in finance."

**SOURCE** — HK Ordinance dates: Library of Congress (2025); Morgan Lewis (2025). JD.com / Ant: 36Kr (2025). GENIUS Act (2025).

## Slide 8 — What's the upside for the world? (benefits + one myth busted)

**ON-SCREEN**
- **What stablecoins make possible:**
- A dollar account for **anyone** — no US bank needed (a refuge from high inflation)
- Cross-border payments in **minutes, for cents** — not days and high fees
- **24/7, programmable** money
- *Myth:* ~~"crypto is private & untraceable"~~ → **Reality: public, traceable, freezable**

**SAY**
"So what's actually *good* about them? Three big things. **One — financial inclusion:** anyone with a phone can hold US dollars without a US bank account, which is a lifeline in countries with runaway inflation. **Two — payments:** sending money across borders takes minutes and costs cents, instead of days and big fees through banks. **Three — it's programmable money** that works around the clock. Now let me bust one myth. People assume crypto is private and untraceable. For mainstream stablecoins, the *opposite* is true. Every transaction lives on a public ledger, forensics firms like Chainalysis trace them, and the issuers can *freeze* your coins — Tether alone froze about 3.3 billion dollars across more than 7,000 wallets between 2023 and 2025. That's bad for privacy — but it's actually good for the world: stolen funds get recovered and criminal money gets blocked."

**SOURCE** — Benefits (inclusion, remittances): Brookings (2025); IMF cross-border data. Traceability & freezes: AMLBot / Cryptonews (2025); Chainalysis (2025–26).

## Slide 9 — When stablecoins aren't stable (the risks)

**ON-SCREEN**
- **The risks:**
- **Runs & depegs** — UST collapsed (2022, ~$40B gone) · USDC briefly fell to **~$0.87** (Mar 2023, bank failure)
- **Reserve quality & transparency** — is the money really there?
- ~**84%** of *fraudulent* on-chain volume flows through stablecoins

**SAY**
"But there's a darker side. Stablecoins can suffer bank-run-style panics. In 2022 the algorithmic coin UST lost its peg and collapsed in days, erasing around 40 billion dollars. In March 2023, even the well-respected USDC briefly fell to about 87 cents when a bank holding its reserves failed — it recovered, but it showed how fragile trust can be. There are also real questions about whether reserves are fully safe and properly audited. And because they move value so easily, stablecoins now show up in a large share of on-chain crime — Chainalysis estimates around 84% of fraudulent on-chain volume. This is exactly why regulators stepped in."

**SOURCE** — UST (2022) & USDC depeg (Mar 2023): widely reported (verify exact figures). Fraud share: Chainalysis, via CryptoTimes (2026).

## Slide 10 — So… good or dangerous?

**ON-SCREEN**
- **Two questions to leave you with:**
- Do stablecoins **strengthen** the dollar — or **spread its risks** worldwide?
- **Stablecoin vs. CBDC** (the digital yuan): who should issue digital money — *companies* or *central banks*?

**SAY**
"So where does this leave us? Stablecoins are clearly useful — cheaper payments, dollar access, more transparency — and they prop up the US dollar. But they also hand a lot of power to a few private companies, and they can fail suddenly. The deeper question is *who should issue digital money*: private firms, through stablecoins, or governments, through central bank digital currencies like China's digital yuan? That's the real battle behind the headlines — and it's only just beginning."

**SOURCE** — Synthesis / CBDC vs stablecoin framing: Brookings (2025); BIS.
